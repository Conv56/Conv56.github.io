Journal Entry #1: Why The Illegal?

There are quite a few reasons why I chose The Illegal as my book, and the title has to be one of them. “Illegal” is usually a word that’s weaponized in real life, and seeing it turned into a human story sounds extremely interesting. On top of this, Lawrence Hill is a big name, as I have read his other books like The Book of Negroes. I admire his handling of displacement and resistance themes. By reading some summaries, I have come to know a little more about the book. The story is about Keita “running” for his life, as he leaves his country for “Freedom State” as an undocumented immigrant. After reading the summary, it almost reassured me that this is the book that I want to read, simply because it greatly relates to the world right now, with the U.S. establishing the ICE (Immigration and Customs Enforcement) to clean out all illegal immigrants, I am very curious to see if the ending of Keita will be similar to what is going to happen in the U.S.

Journal Entry #2: Character Development

First Third

Keita Ali emerges as a driven youth, obsessive about running. Hiding his “Meb Supremes” in his knapsack shows his ambition and defiance: he was so in love with his Meb Supremes … he had left the house with them hidden in his knapsack (Hill 13).

As an adult, winning under an alias brings pride and fear: Worry framed his eyes and furrowed his brow when he sees his photo listed as “Roger Bannister” (Hill 144).

Second Third

In AfricTown, locals chant Keita Ali, go for gold! and offer oranges—giving him belonging beyond survival: There was no gold to run for… Children stood by the roadside and clapped (Hill 285).

Mrs. Beech’s kindness after his collapse—Do you know who you are? … Yes … Keita Ali (Hill 206)—teaches him trust and interdependence.

Final Third

Keita confronts Minister Calder: Don’t you have something for me? He flees barriers mid-race—He hurdled the barrier and kept running (Hill 322)—illustrating unshakable will to protect loved ones and challenge injustice.

Journal Entry #3: Novel Background

Lawrence Hill’s parents were civil‑rights activists: his father Daniel Hill led the Ontario Human Rights Commission; his mother Donna Hill fought anti‑discrimination law. Hill’s journalism and volunteer work, plus a childhood dream of Olympic running, shaped The Illegal.

“obscene and heartbreaking” (Hill — exact page TBD)

Published mid‑2010s amid a record refugee crisis—60 million displaced (UN 2015)—Hill’s fictional Zantoroland and Freedom State mirror real‑world Syria/Rwanda and ICE/Australia. AfricTown echoes Africville’s tragic history.

Journal Entry #4: Resilience

Keita’s resilience peaks when he confronts dehumanization: I am not asking for mercy, Minister. I am asking for rights. I am not an animal (Hill 376). His refusal reframes resilience as active defiance.

Running becomes protest: hurdling barriers symbolizes resistance to unjust systems. Hill suggests personal grit combined with collective solidarity sparks social change.

Journal Entry #5: Literary Lenses

Socio‑Economic Lens

AfricTown’s container homes—It looked poorer than Zantoroland… (Hill 182)—expose capitalism’s role in migrant exploitation.

Psychological Lens

After his father’s kidnapping, Keita rereads Yoyo’s final words—The time has come. Call the marathon agent (Hill 46)—as a coping ritual, showing memory’s power amid trauma.

Archetypal Lens

Keita’s arc follows the hero’s journey: call to adventure (Marathon win), belly of the whale (AfricTown), atonement (Calder showdown).

Journal Entry #6: Text-to-Text Connections

The Hamilton Mixtape: “Immigrants (We Get the Job Done)”

Miranda’s anthem—“We jump over walls or float on rafts / We built you a castle”—mirrors Keita’s flight and rebuild.

Disney’s Encanto (2021)

Abuela Alma’s refugee backstory echoes Keita’s exile: trauma, loss, and rebuilding community under pressure.

Childish Gambino: “This Is America”

Glover’s dance‑and‑violence imagery parallels Hill’s critique of oppression and the need to confront hidden brutality.

Journal Entry #7: Sentence Starter

“I think the relationship between Keita and Mrs. Ivernia Beech is interesting because…”

I think their bond shows resilience demands trust. When Mrs. Beech asks, Do you know who you are? (Hill 112), Keita reclaims his name and self-worth, proving strength lies in caring connections.

Journal Entry #8: Ending Reflection

The ending is both triumphant and open-ended. After Keita’s victory, he realizes he ran not for speed, but for survival (Hill 390). Yet eyeing a new race, Hill reminds us justice is ongoing—one victory sparks the next movement.

Journal Entry #9: Why Teach The Illegal

  1. Humanizes Refugees: Transforms statistics into Keita’s lived story.
  2. Prompts Critical Discussion: Sharp critiques of dehumanizing language and policies.
  3. Models Resilience: Demonstrates moral courage and the spark for social change.
